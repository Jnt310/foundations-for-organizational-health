# 01 — Foundations Charter

**Body of Work:** Foundations for Organizational Health™  
**Version:** 0.1  
**Status:** Draft Ratified  
**Date Ratified:** June 25, 2026  
**Founder:** Jessica Mastrangelo, MBA, LPC, NCC  

## 1. Legacy Statement

I want to be remembered as someone who helped heal our broken system for those of us who work in it so that we could heal those we entered this field to heal.

## 2. First Principle

Heal the people and the systems that provide care so they can better heal the people they serve.

## 3. Mission

To advance the science and practice of organizational health by developing evidence-informed frameworks, assessments, and tools that help mission-driven organizations create healthier workplaces, strengthen ethical leadership, foster resilient cultures, and improve the care they provide to the people and communities they serve.

## 4. Purpose

Foundations for Organizational Health™ exists to strengthen the conditions that allow organizations and the people within them to care sustainably, ethically, and effectively.

## 5. Scope

The body of work begins in behavioral healthcare because behavioral healthcare organizations experience many organizational stressors at high intensity: burnout, turnover, financial pressure, compliance burden, documentation burden, ethical risk, leadership strain, and workforce instability.

Behavioral healthcare is the first application area and proving ground. The broader philosophy may later extend to healthcare, human services, education, nonprofit organizations, and other mission-driven helping professions.

## 6. Foundational Hypothesis

Healthy outcomes emerge from healthy ecosystems. Sustainable organizations are built when leadership, culture, people, systems, stewardship, ethics, and purpose are intentionally aligned.

## 7. Theory of Change

Sustainable improvements in care occur when organizations intentionally strengthen leadership, culture, workforce capacity, operational systems, financial stewardship, quality, compliance, and innovation as an interconnected ecosystem. Improvements in one domain are unlikely to endure if the surrounding organizational conditions remain unhealthy.

## 8. Core Beliefs

### 8.1 Organizations are designed to produce their current results.
Persistent problems such as turnover, poor morale, inconsistent quality, or ethical drift are rarely isolated individual failures. They are signals from the system.

### 8.2 Culture is infrastructure.
Culture is not an employee perk or soft concept. It is the operating environment in which clinical, financial, operational, ethical, and quality systems succeed or fail.

### 8.3 Leadership creates conditions.
Leadership is not merely decision-making authority. Leadership is the responsibility to create the conditions in which people, systems, and care can thrive.

### 8.4 Compliance is a floor, not a ceiling.
An organization can be compliant and still be unhealthy. Passing audits does not guarantee trust, ethical practice, staff well-being, or sustainable care.

### 8.5 Financial strength without organizational health is temporary.
Strong revenue and margins cannot indefinitely compensate for poor culture, ineffective leadership, workforce instability, or ethical erosion.

### 8.6 People should not have to sacrifice themselves to provide excellent care.
Organizations have a responsibility to build conditions where mission-driven work can be sustained without chronic harm to the people doing the work.

### 8.7 Every person holds organizational wisdom.
Position determines responsibility, not the value of a person’s perspective. The people closest to the work often understand the work in ways leaders cannot see from above.

### 8.8 Learning is a strategic capability.
Healthy organizations do not hide mistakes. They learn from them, improve systems, and strengthen trust through transparency.

## 9. Culture Principles

### We do the hard work.
We address difficult truths, conversations, decisions, and system failures even when it is uncomfortable.

### Mistakes are data.
Mistakes are expected in complex systems. Repeating avoidable mistakes without learning is not. Healthy organizations treat mistakes as opportunities to improve systems and strengthen practice.

### Every voice matters.
Every role contributes to the mission. No title makes a person’s opinion more valuable as a human contribution. Expertise matters, responsibility differs, but dignity and insight are distributed throughout the organization.

### Curiosity before judgment.
We seek to understand what happened, what conditions contributed to it, and what can be improved before assigning blame.

### Psychological safety creates accountability.
People must be able to speak honestly about concerns, risks, mistakes, burnout, and ethical issues without fear of retaliation. This increases accountability rather than weakening it.

### Stewardship over ownership.
Every person is responsible for leaving the organization healthier than they found it.

## 10. Leadership Philosophy

Leadership is not about having the best answers. Leadership is about creating an environment where the best answers can emerge from the collective wisdom of the organization.

Healthy leadership requires humility, courage, accountability, transparency, and the willingness to listen across positional boundaries.

## 11. Design Principles for the Body of Work

1. Seek truth over convenience.
2. Measure what matters, not only what is easy to measure.
3. Diagnose systems before assigning blame.
4. Test assumptions with evidence.
5. Welcome disagreement because it improves the work.
6. Revise thinking when evidence warrants it.
7. Protect the dignity of every person affected by the work.
8. Build for sustainability, not short-term performance.
9. Leave every idea clearer than it was found.
10. Remain intellectually humble.

## 12. Ethical Commitments

Foundations for Organizational Health™ will not reduce organizational health to productivity, profitability, or compliance alone. Financial sustainability matters, but it is not the sole measure of health. Organizational health must include trust, ethical practice, learning, psychological safety, leadership capacity, workforce sustainability, operational excellence, and care quality.

## 13. Version 0.1 Status

This Charter is ratified as Version 0.1. It is not final. It establishes the first coherent foundation for future development.
